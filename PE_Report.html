Evaluating PATH (Physical Activity to Thrive & Heal) Training in Schools: Preliminary Findings

Author

PDY

Program Context and Objectives

Physical fitness training is more important than ever due to children being confined indoors during COVID and the ongoing childhood obesity epidemic. To address this, the State of Greatlakesia launched PATH (Physical Activity to Thrive & Heal), a program designed to improve physical activity and well-being among students. PATH offers training for a variety of school professionals in both synchronous and asynchronous formats, allowing flexibility for participants.

Although a randomized control trial is planned to evaluate student outcomes, preliminary feedback is being collected from teachers before and after completing the training. These early data allow the program to assess differences between synchronous and asynchronous modalities and provide a quasi-experimental perspective on training effectiveness.

Data Collection and Analysis Approach

Surveys were administered to school staff before and after the PATH training, and participants were categorized into four roles:

  • Classroom Educators
  • Physical Education Teachers
  • Administrators
  • Other Student Support Roles

The PATH training is informed by the STEP (Standards for Training, Exercise, & Physical Education) Institute’s five categories of physical fitness:

  • Adapted Physical Activity & Technology (APAT)
  • Strength
  • Cardio
  • Flexibility & Mobility
  • Diet & Nutrition

In addition, Teamwork and Cooperation was included to complement the physical domains by emphasizing collaboration, communication, and effective group participation. Survey scores were aggregated into six indices corresponding to these categories.

Random assignment was not possible. To control for individual differences, pre- and post-surveys were paired using Levenshtein distance, and the matches were verified manually (Levenshtein 1966). The Qualtrics survey data were prepared for analysis with a data dictionary incorporated into the cleaning process (Lewis 2022).

Assumptions and Reliability

Normality checks on the difference scores (post − pre) showed mild positive skew and near-normal kurtosis. Given the moderate sample size and the use of Welch’s t-test, which is robust to unequal variances and moderately non-normal distributions, the assumptions for the independent-samples comparisons are considered reasonable.

All indices demonstrated acceptable to good internal consistency, with Cronbach’s alpha values indicating that the items within each scale cohered well. This suggests that the composite scores reliably capture the underlying constructs of Strength, Cardio, Diet/Nutrition, Adapted Activities, Flexibility/Mobility, and Teamwork. Consequently, these indices provide a stable basis for examining pre-post changes and differences across professional roles or delivery methods.

Analysis

Comparing Outcomes Across Training Delivery Modalities

Welch two-sample t-tests were used to compare asynchronous(n = 2016) and synchronous(n =384) training groups across all five STEP categories and Teamwork and Cooperation. No statistically significant differences were observed (p values ranged from 0.380 to 0.915), indicating that outcomes did not vary meaningfully by training delivery format. Accordingly, the two groups were combined for subsequent analyses to increase statistical power and simplify interpretation.

Differences in Training Gains Across Professional Roles

Linear regression models were estimated with pre–post change scores as the outcome and role indicators as predictors, using Physical Education Teachers as the reference group. Robust (HC1) standard errors were applied to account for potential heteroskedasticity, following applied econometric guidance on nonstandard standard error issues(Angrist and Pischke 2009).

Results indicate positive differences in training gains across all outcome domains for Classroom Educators, Administrators, and Student Support staff. Classroom Educators showed improvements in Adapted Physical Activity & Technology (0.191), Cardio (0.175), Diet & Nutrition (0.191), Flexibility & Mobility (0.197), Strength (0.191), and Teamwork & Cooperation (0.199), with Administrators and Student Support staff exhibiting similar patterns. These findings reflect broadly positive gains with modest variation across professional roles, consistent with differences in baseline exposure to PE-related practices.

After excluding participants with prior PE instruction, gains were larger across all domains. Classroom Educators demonstrated pronounced improvements in Adapted Physical Activity & Technology (0.277), Cardio (0.257), Diet & Nutrition (0.271), Flexibility & Mobility (0.284), Strength (0.270), and Teamwork & Cooperation (0.285). Administrators and Student Support staff also showed substantial gains, with the largest effects in Strength (0.254*** for Administrators; 0.326*** for Student Support staff) and Flexibility & Mobility (0.302*** for Administrators; 0.316*** for Student Support). These results highlight heterogeneity in responsiveness, with participants lacking prior PE exposure showing the greatest improvements, consistent with the training addressing gaps not typically covered in traditional PE instruction.
