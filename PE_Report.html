Evaluating PATH (Physical Activity to Thrive & Heal) Training in Schools: Preliminary Findings

Author

Paul Yuergens

Program Context and Objectives

Physical fitness training is particularly important given children’s reduced physical activity during COVID-related school closures and increased levels of childhood obesity(Lange et al. 2021). To address this, the State of Greatlakesia launched PATH (Physical Activity to Thrive & Heal), a program designed to improve physical activity and well-being among students. PATH offers training for a variety of school professionals in both synchronous and asynchronous formats, allowing flexibility for participants.

Although a randomized control trial is planned to evaluate student outcomes, preliminary feedback is being collected from teachers before and after completing the training. These early data allow the program to assess differences between synchronous and asynchronous modalities and provide a quasi-experimental perspective on training effectiveness.

Data Collection and Analysis Approach

Surveys were administered to school staff before and after the PATH training, and participants were categorized into four roles:

  • Classroom Educators (CE)
  • Physical Education Teachers (PET)
  • Administrators (ADMIN)
  • Other Student Support Roles (OSS)

The PATH training is informed by the STEP (Standards for Training, Exercise, & Physical Education) Institute’s five categories of physical fitness:

  • Adapted Physical Activity & Technology (APAT)
  • Strength
  • Cardio
  • Flexibility & Mobility
  • Diet & Nutrition

In addition, Teamwork and Cooperation was included to complement the physical domains by emphasizing collaboration, communication, and effective group participation. Survey scores were aggregated into six indices corresponding to these categories.

Random assignment was not possible. To control for individual differences, pre- and post-surveys were paired using Levenshtein distance, and the matches were verified manually (Levenshtein 1966). The Qualtrics survey data were prepared for analysis with a data dictionary incorporated into the cleaning process (Lewis 2022).

Assumptions and Reliability

Normality checks on the difference scores (post − pre) showed nearly symmetric distributions (skewness = -0.003 to 0.17) and near-normal kurtosis (2.89-3.06), with Shapiro-Wilk tests indicating significant departures from normality (all p < .001). However, given the large sample sizes (n = 2,016 asynchronous, n = 384 synchronous), Welch’s independent-samples t-test was appropriate for comparing the two groups.

All indices demonstrated good to excellent internal consistency (Cronbach’s α = 0.81–0.91), with the Teamwork scale showing particularly strong reliability (α = 0.91). Average inter-item correlations ranged from 0.59 to 0.60, indicating appropriate item homogeneity without excessive redundancy. Item-total correlations were consistently strong (r > 0.65), and no items required removal. These reliability estimates suggest that the composite scores reliably capture the underlying constructs of Strength, Cardio, Diet/Nutrition, Adapted Activities, Flexibility/Mobility, and Teamwork, providing a stable basis for examining pre-post changes and differences across professional roles or delivery methods.

Analysis

Comparing Outcomes Across Training Delivery Modalities

Welch two-sample t-tests were used to compare asynchronous (n = 2016) and synchronous (n = 384) training groups across all five STEP categories and Teamwork and Cooperation. No statistically significant differences were observed (p values ranged from 0.076 to 0.976), indicating that outcomes did not vary meaningfully by training delivery format. Accordingly, the two groups were combined for subsequent analyses to increase statistical power and simplify interpretation.

Differences in Training Gains Across Professional Roles (EFFECTIVENESS)

Linear regression models were estimated with pre–post change scores as the outcome and role indicators as predictors, using Physical Education Teachers as the reference group. Robust (HC1) standard errors were applied to account for potential heteroskedasticity, following applied econometric guidance on nonstandard standard error issues(Angrist and Pischke 2009).

All professional roles demonstrated significant pre-post improvements across outcome domains. Physical Education Teachers (reference group) showed gains ranging from 0.185 (APAT) to 0.243 (Cardio and Strength). Compared to PE Teachers, Classroom Educators demonstrated additional improvements across all domains, with differences ranging from 0.124 (Strength, p < .01) to 0.196 (APAT, p < .001). Administrators and Student Support staff exhibited similar patterns, showing significantly greater gains than PE Teachers in most domains. These findings reflect variation in baseline exposure to PE-related practices, with non-PE professionals showing larger improvements consistent with addressing knowledge gaps not typically covered in their prior training or professional experience.

Among participants without prior PE instruction, the pattern of differential gains became more pronounced. Physical Education Teachers in this subsample improved by 0.181–0.266 across domains. Relative to these PE Teachers, Classroom Educators showed additional gains of 0.157–0.24 (p < .001 across all domains), Administrators showed additional gains of 0.133–0.252 (p < .05 to p < .001), and Student Support staff showed additional gains of 0.138–0.265 (p < .01 to p < .001). The largest relative gains appeared in Flexibility & Mobility for Student Support staff (additional 0.265, p < .001) and Administrators (additional 0.252, p < .001). These results highlight that participants lacking prior PE exposure showed the greatest responsiveness to training, with the program effectively addressing content gaps not typically covered in traditional PE instruction.

To aid interpretation of the regression results, Table 4 presents descriptive pre- and post-training mean scores by professional role and outcome domain. Visual inspection of these changes reveals an important pattern: while all professional roles showed statistically significant improvements, the magnitude of gains was modest across all groups. Physical Education Teachers, who entered with higher baseline scores (M = 2.67-2.92), demonstrated small improvements (0.15-0.28 points, representing approximately 4-7% of the scale range). Classroom Educators, Administrators, and Student Support staff—who started at similar baseline levels (M = 2.74-2.89)—showed somewhat larger gains (0.38-0.49 points, approximately 10-12% of scale range), though these too represent modest practical effects. This pattern underscores that with large samples (n = 2,400), statistical significance does not necessarily indicate large practical effects. However, non-PE professionals consistently gained more than PE Teachers, suggesting the training addressed specific knowledge gaps for those with limited prior PE exposure, even if the magnitude of improvement was moderate.

Discussion

This study examined the effectiveness of the PATH training program across 2,400 school personnel using a pre-post design. Three key findings emerged.

First, training delivery modality (synchronous vs. asynchronous) did not significantly affect outcomes (all p > .05), suggesting flexible implementation without compromising effectiveness.

Second, while all professional roles demonstrated statistically significant improvements across the six competency domains (all p < .001), practical significance was modest. Gains averaged 0.35-0.36 points on the 4-point scale (approximately 9% of scale range). With large samples, even small effects achieve statistical significance, highlighting the importance of interpreting results beyond p-values alone.

Third, non-PE professionals (Classroom Educators, Administrators, Student Support) showed significantly greater gains than Physical Education Teachers (additional 0.12-0.20 points, p < .01 to p < .001). While statistically significant, this difference represents approximately 3-5% of the scale range—a modest practical difference. This pattern suggests the training addressed knowledge gaps for those with limited PE background, though the magnitude of benefit was small. Whether such gains translate to meaningful changes in practice or student outcomes remains an open question.

These findings illustrate the tension between statistical and practical significance in large-scale professional development research. Future work should examine whether training effects persist over time and whether they lead to observable changes in professional practice.
